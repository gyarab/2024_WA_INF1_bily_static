Coherence Scanning Interferometry

Truly in-depth 3D optical surface characterization and analysis.

Coherence Scanning Interferometry is a measurement method used in the fabrication and analysis of optical, semiconductor and other surfaces. This method utilizes one of the two quantum properties of light which is it's wave form. We take for example the Michelson interferometry. It uses the interference of two light beams to analyse the topography and characteristics of a surface.

Key to this method is a coherent light source. A coherent light sources is a single point light source (for this application typically a white light source) in which all of the photons have the same energy, frequency and wavelength phase. To create a base reference to compare the interfered measurement beam with. This is done either by having two separate light sources or as in the Michelson interferometer, we split a beam of light comning from one source into two separate beams using a beam splitter. Primary beam, which is used as a reference, is sent on a path perpendicular to the sample being scanned, it reflects off of a flat reference mirror and is sent back to the beam splitter. In the beam splitter it combines with the secondary beam used to scan the surface of the object. The two beams interfere with each other and this interference is then captured on the detector in form of Z-axis measurement of the profile of the surface compared to the flat reference mirror.

Other key component is a photodetector. For this any CMOS, CCD or other detectors can be used.
